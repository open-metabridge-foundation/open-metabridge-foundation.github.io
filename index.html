What is the Open MetaBridge Foundation?

The goal of the Open MetaBridge Foundation (OMBF) is to establish a set of protocols that are interoperable and agnostic. These protocols will facilitate the connection between physical items and their corresponding digital counterparts.

Furthermore, the OMBF seeks to create an inclusive ecosystem for implementing partners, promoting collaboration and ongoing development.

Core Protocol

At an abstract level, the core Open MetaBridge Protocol (OMBP) comprises the following steps:

  1. Registration: The physical item's creator registers the item and an independent claiming code on the distributed ledger.
  2. Out-of-band trade: The creator and user agree on a trade outside of the protocol. The creator then hands the claiming code to the user.
  3. Claiming: The user utilizes the claiming code to create a non-fungible token (NFT) for the physical item.

During the Registration and Claiming steps, the creator and user respectively must prove physical possession of the item. The authenticity of the item can be verified during the trading step or any subsequent physical transfers of the item.

Open Source

The OMBF maintains all of its protocols in an open-source manner, allowing community members to contribute to their development. The organization operates as a non-profit, and all of its work is made public. Additionally, anyone is welcome to attend OMBF meetings as they are open to the public.

Interoperability

Creating agnostic protocols that can work with any smart contract platform is at the core of OMBF's mission. The organization aims to develop protocols that are not limited to any specific type of distributed ledger or blockchain, and could even be implemented with a centralized database.

Security

The OMBF's protocols rely on contemporary cryptographic primitives and are publicly auditable, thanks to the organization's open-source nature. Ensuring the security and future-proofing of these protocols is a crucial value of the OMBF, as it aims to foster long-term, established trust in both the protocols themselves and their implementations.
